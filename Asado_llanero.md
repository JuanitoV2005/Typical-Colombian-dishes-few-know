# **Asado llanero**

## *Synopsis*

<p align="justify">
The orinoquia region is also known as “llanos orientales”. This region is a huge extension of open savannahs that include a part of Colombia and Venezuela. It is a territory with a lot of rivers and different kinds of animals, such as the cachama, palomera y payana. The climate is a relationship between drought and rain, and the native vegetation includes the yopo tree which is useful green and dry to produce fire for cooking. 
</p>

<p align="justify">  
Historically, the region has been defined by three related elements, cattle ranching, the horse and fire. These are not simple economic elements, in fact the elements represent the base of the llanero cultural identity. The Orinoco gastronomy reflects this reality: dishes like mamona, chiguiro, tungos, hallacas llaneras and casabe are not aleatory or isolated recipes. Rather the expressions showing a way of life built the communitarian work and the cattle ranching.
</p>

## *Review*

<p align="justify">
Asado llanero does not have indigenous ancient roots. Before Spanish colonization, the diet of indigenous peoples in the region was largely plant-based, with minimal consumption of meat and fat and no tradition of domestic cattle. Studies of the pre-Hispanic diet describe a dietary regime oriented toward balance with the environment. Very different in the present intensive meat consumption that we associate with the llanos.  
</p>

<p align="justify">
The cattle arrived with a Spanish colonization. What we understand as “llanero tradition” is the result of a process of mixing and imposition of an European productive logic in the landscape. The asado culture, far from being ancestral, consolidated from the cattle ranching introduced during the conquest. For this reason, it is clearer to say that the llanero tradition is a few centuries old.
</p>

<p align="justify">
But if we want to understand the difference between carne llanera and asado llanero, it is necessary to talk about the difference in the foods and techniques of llanero cuisine. We begin with the term “asado” which refers to any kind of meat placed on a grill and cooked with the heat of the embers. It is a universal method. The asado llanero is different, because it is a method of the savanna: the meat is not placed on a horizontal grill but is instead skewered on metal or wooden rods around the fire. In this way the meat cooks upright, slowly through the indirect fire of the embers, and seasoned with salt.
</p>

<p align="justify">
Finally, “carne a la llanero or mamona” is a dish that is the result of applying the asado llanero method to veal. The word mamona refers to a young cow or calf still of nursing age, for instance, the animal between 9 and 11 months old. The meat is cut according to a traditional four cut system with names specific to the region.  La OSA: jowl, jaw, tongue and neck. Los TEMBLADORES: all the brisket meat. La RAYA: Hindquarters, haunches, thighs and tail. Finally, La GARZA: udders. In addition to these four cuts, other cuts are also used. The goal is to achieve tenderness in the meat, which is why it differs somewhat from other methods.
</p>

<p align="justify">
Beyond the savanna, carne a la llanera has become a gastronomic ambassador of the plains in the big cities. In Bogotá, the restaurant Mona Mamona run by the llanero Luis Camargo, who brought to the capital his original venue from Yopal, Casanare has taken the authentic asado a la vara to an urban public. His purpose, according to Camargo, was not "to open one more business, but to be ambassadors of an identity": unlike the grills that lighten and thin the meat to have it ready in forty minutes, Mona Mamona upholds the slow, five-hour asado, made the way it is made in the plains. In this way, the dish is consolidated as a symbol of regional identity beyond its territory of origin.
</p>

<p align="justify">
The preparation of carne a la llanera has a simple seasoning, but it needs patience with the fire. In its most traditional version, the one from the pure plains, the meat is seasoned only with salt. A lot of salt was rubbed on the night before. People believe that this is how real carne a la llanera is made. There are also other, more urban versions. In these, the meat is left in a marinade from the night before with spices and a little beer. Some preparations from the region use chicha de ahuyama or guarulo. In all cases, the meat is not cooked over direct fire. It is cooked with the heat of the embers. The embers are not made stronger, because this way the meat does not dry out.
</p>

<p align="justify">
After that, the cuts are put on wooden rods or sticks. The sticks are placed at an angle around the fire. The fire is round and it is made in a special hole. Another option is to use a wooden or iron structure called el burro. The cooking is slow and it takes several hours. The rods are turned from time to time, so the meat becomes golden on all sides. During the cooking, the meat is not pierced or cut. This way it keeps its juices. At the end, the meat is served on a table covered with plantain leaves. It is served with cassava, plantain, papa criolla or roasted potatoes, and the salad you prefer. It is a dish to share.
</p>
